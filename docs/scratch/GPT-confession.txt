Now your actual question — are the three structurally anti-recurrence or recurrence-biased? Neither, and the reason matters more than the answer.
Merges are selected by identifying the pair with the lowest overall penalty score. P_s = 1 − mean(top-k similarity), so high similarity → low penalty → merged first. A singular event is dissimilar to its neighbors → high P_s → merged last → survives at highest fidelity. So StreamForest preferentially destroys redundancy and preserves distinctness. Your "similarity clustering buries singulars" is false for StreamForest. Nxcode
But because adjacency is a hard constraint, recurrence-across-days is invisible to P_s — it never compares Monday to Tuesday. What P_s compresses is local redundancy (an hour at a desk), not recurrence. The penalties are anti-redundancy and recurrence-agnostic.
The consequence is the finding: the recurrence bias is a property of the abstraction operator, not of trees. StreamForest has no abstraction axis, therefore no recurrence bias, therefore needs no bypass. RAPTOR has one and buries singulars via forced soft cluster assignment. The moment you add cross-time abstraction — which any semantic tier requires — you acquire the bias. So your bypass isn't a fix to a tree; it's constitutive of cross-time consolidation. You cannot cite StreamForest as evidence the bias exists, and you no longer need to.
One more, unprompted, and it's free ammunition: P_t = 1 − (d_i+d_{i+1})/2 with d_i = (t_q − t_i)/t_q. As t_q grows, d_i → 1 for every fixed t_i, so P_t → 0 uniformly and the temporal term stops discriminating. At month scale, everything older than a few hours has identical temporal penalty and the term is dead — PEMF silently degenerates to similarity + merge-count. 3. Paper-worthiness
The system paper is not writable now, and I'd have said that even without the crowding. Its cells are occupied by five-plus systems — the four we debated plus PyraVid, FluxMem, Hermes, StreamChat — and its motivating premise has, on the only evidence that exists, ≤1.4 points of headroom on the benchmark where the strongest competitor sits. Proposing to train something expensive to win 1.4 points against training-free baselines is a rejection, not a paper.
But "≤1.4" is not evidence the projection is lossless. It's evidence the instrument can't resolve. And you now have that from two directions, in published numbers, in the SOTA papers' own ablations, misread by their authors as robustness. That's not a gap in the literature. It's a mistake in the literature, which is a better thing to own than a gap, because gaps get filled by whoever's faster and mistakes have to be argued.
So the shape is a measurement paper: what these benchmarks can and cannot resolve, the evidence already sitting in the SOTA ablations, and a probe that separates "captions suffice" from "captions suffice for this eval." It's unclaimed because both papers ran the ablation and read it backwards. It's cheap — you measure existing systems, no month-span training. And it's falsifiable both ways with both ways publishable: if captions suffice, the latent direction is dead and that finding saves the field real money; if they don't, every SOTA number is measuring the wrong thing.
Now the part I won't soften. The probe may come back "the projection is fine." Both convergent numbers are equally consistent with "the benchmarks are blind" and with "perception is solved for anything anyone asks." You've been designing, for six rounds, a solution to a problem whose existence is currently unevidenced — and I helped, at length, without once noticing that. ODV-Bench's 20-point gap is your best counter-evidence and it's confounded three ways. Your probe also has a hole you haven't addressed: it must be usage-derived to survive the "adversarial and unrepresentative" rejection, and nobody knows what people actually ask of month-old video memory. That's unmeasured, it's upstream of your probe's design, and it's a second measurement problem hiding under the first.
And the immunity is conditional: the probe is reviewer-proof only if you don't ship a method that wins on it. The moment you add certification, you're building the eval you win on. That's a real cost, it's the cost of the honest version, and it means the measurement paper and the system paper cannot be the same paper.
I'm not going to tell you certification is the answer. Certification is a solution whose problem is, right now, unevidenced.
